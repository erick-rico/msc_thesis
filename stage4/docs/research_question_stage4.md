# Research questions – Stage 4 context

## Thesis core research questions

- **RQ0.** What synoptic and sub-seasonal circulation patterns are associated with extreme PM₂.₅ and PM₁₀ episodes over the Valley of Mexico, and how are these patterns modulated by ENSO?

- **RQ1.** Which circulation regimes are most frequently associated with extreme PM₂.₅ and PM₁₀ events, and how do their composite signatures differ?

- **RQ2.** What are the typical boundary-layer stability/ventilation characteristics during PM episodes versus non-episode days?

- **RQ3.** Do monthly and intraseasonal composites reveal background conditions that “precondition” PM episodes beyond event-day anomalies?

- **RQ4.** How do episode frequency and composite patterns differ between El Niño, La Niña, and neutral conditions?

---

## Specific questions for this stage

- **1.** Can the monthly circulation contrasts established in Stage 3 be reduced to a smaller number of robust seasonal stories without losing the main physical signal?

- **2.** Which selected windows best represent the late-winter benchmark, the transition-season case, and the wet-season contrast for PM10 and PM2.5?

- **3.** Do polluted episode-onset days cluster into a small set of recurrent synoptic regimes when analyzed separately for PM10 and PM2.5?

- **4.** How does the circulation evolve from the days before onset to the days after onset, and does this evolution differ between February, May, and July?

- **5.** Does PM2.5 retain more organized nonwinter structure than PM10 when examined through regime extraction and lagged composites?

- **6.** Are the selected synoptic stories physically consistent with precipitation behavior, lower-tropospheric circulation, and lower-tropospheric moisture structure?

---

## Rationale

Stage 4 extends the monthly contrast framework established in Stage 3 by moving from:

- **12 monthly diagnostics** → to **3 representative seasonal windows**
- **monthly p90 versus p10 contrasts** → to **event-onset-centered synoptic prototypes and lag evolution**

This stage is designed to answer a more focused interpretive question:

> Which seasonal windows best summarize the polluted-event story, and are those stories dynamically and physically consistent?

The point is not to multiply diagnostics, but to reduce complexity in a way that prepares the final ENSO-modulation stage.

---

## Analytical structure

Stage 4 proceeds through four main steps:

1. **Seasonal synthesis of the Stage 3 monthly story**

   - condense monthly diagnostics into a few robust seasonal narratives
   - formally select February, May, and July

2. **Light regime extraction from episode-onset days**

   - one onset day per episode
   - pollutant-specific k-means clustering using Z500 anomalies
   - regime composites and regime-frequency summaries

3. **Lag composites around event onset**

   - lags from −3 to +2 days
   - February, May, and July examined separately
   - PM10 and PM2.5 compared in parallel

4. **Targeted physical mechanism check**

   - daily precipitation around onset
   - 850 hPa circulation composites
   - 850 hPa specific humidity composites
   - lagged mechanism diagnostics over the Valley of Mexico

---

## Onset-day framework

Stage 4 uses a strict event-centered sampling rule:

> **day 0 = first day of the p90 episode**

This choice:

- avoids overcounting persistent episodes,
- links the clustering step directly to event initiation,
- and aligns the lag analysis with precursor-versus-persistence interpretation.

---

## Temporal and spatial scope

- **PM data**
  - Daily city-mean PM10 and PM2.5
  - Period: 2012–2024

- **Atmospheric fields**
  - NARR reanalysis
  - 500 hPa geopotential height and winds
  - daily precipitation
  - 850 hPa geopotential height, winds, and specific humidity

- **Lag window**
  - −3 to +2 days relative to onset

- **Focus windows**
  - February
  - May
  - July

- **Spatial domain**
  - Mexico-centered domain consistent with earlier stages

---

## Intended contribution of Stage 4

Stage 4 is intended to provide:

- a compact seasonal interpretation of the monthly contrasts from Stage 3,
- a pollutant-specific onset-day regime framework,
- a life-cycle view of polluted-event evolution,
- and a limited but physically meaningful consistency test using precipitation, 850 hPa flow, and SH850.

This stage therefore creates the **seasonal and synoptic synthesis** needed before asking the final climate-modulation question.

---

## Limitations and scope boundaries

Stage 4 is intentionally limited.

It does **not** aim to:

- build a full climatological weather-pattern classification,
- resolve detailed aerosol chemistry or source apportionment,
- perform full moisture-transport diagnostics,
- or attribute observed patterns directly to climate variability.

Additionally:

- k-means is used here as a practical summarizing tool, not as a final or unique truth about circulation regimes
- lag composites represent average evolution, not every individual episode pathway
- precipitation and SH850 are treated as physical-consistency diagnostics rather than standalone causal proof
- 850-hPa level is used here as a lower-tropospheric diagnostic of regional flow and moisture transport, while acknowledging that over high-elevation terrain it may not represent near-surface conditions directly

These boundaries are important to keep Stage 4 focused and to leave the ENSO-modulation analysis clearly reserved for Stage 5.

---

## Transition to Stage 5

The main role of Stage 4 is to identify the event stories that are worth carrying forward.

If February, May, and July do summarize:

- the late-winter benchmark,
- the transition-season behavior,
- and the wet-season PM10–PM2.5 contrast,

then Stage 5 can ask the next question cleanly:

> Are these seasonal synoptic stories stable across ENSO phases, or are they systematically modulated by large-scale climate variability?
