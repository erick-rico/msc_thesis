# Research questions – Stage 5 context

## Thesis core research questions

- **RQ0.** What synoptic and sub-seasonal circulation patterns are associated with extreme PM₂.₅ and PM₁₀ episodes over the Valley of Mexico, and how are these patterns modulated by ENSO?

- **RQ1.** Which circulation regimes are most frequently associated with extreme PM₂.₅ and PM₁₀ events, and how do their composite signatures differ?

- **RQ2.** What are the typical boundary-layer stability/ventilation characteristics during PM episodes versus non-episode days?

- **RQ3.** Do monthly and intraseasonal composites reveal background conditions that “precondition” PM episodes beyond event-day anomalies?

- **RQ4.** How do episode frequency and composite patterns differ between El Niño, La Niña, and neutral conditions?

---

## Specific questions for this stage

- **1.** Can the final thesis story be reorganized around **seasonal onset-based composites** rather than month-by-month diagnostics without losing the main physical signal?

- **2.** Which seasonal roles best summarize the polluted-event problem for PM10 and PM2.5?

  - DJF as dry benchmark?
  - MAM as transition season?
  - JJA as wet-season contrast?
  - SON as drying-season reorganization / completeness?

- **3.** Do seasonal lag composites show coherent differences in **preconditioning, onset organization, and short persistence** across DJF, MAM, JJA, and SON?

- **4.** Are the seasonal onset signals statistically distinguishable from **seasonal non-onset background conditions** at day 0 and at a key precursor lag?

- **5.** Does seasonal precipitation context support the benchmark / transition / wet-season / drying-season interpretation used in the final narrative?

- **6.** How do **onset frequency**, **onset-day synoptic structure**, and **WHO exceedance behavior** differ across El Niño, Neutral, and La Niña conditions?

- **7.** Which ENSO-conditioned results are statistically robust, and which are better treated as descriptive support rather than central claims?

---

## Rationale

Stage 5 closes the staged analysis framework by moving from:

- **monthly contrasts** → to **seasonal onset stories**
- **seasonal synthesis** → to **seasonal statistical support**
- **seasonal physical interpretation** → to **climate-phase modulation**

This stage is therefore designed to answer the final interpretive question:

> Can the polluted-event story over the Valley of Mexico be summarized through a small number of seasonally interpretable onset patterns, and does ENSO modify the frequency, structure, or burden of those patterns?

The point is not to reopen every branch of the thesis, but to produce a compact final framework that is ready to be written into Results and Discussion.

---

## Analytical structure

Stage 5 proceeds through five main steps:

1. **Seasonal descriptive framework before onset analysis**

   - summarize onset counts, burden context, and precipitation background
   - define DJF / MAM / JJA / SON narrative roles

2. **Seasonal onset-day and lag composites**

   - day-0 composites by season and pollutant
   - lag composites from −3 to +2 days
   - compact seasonal lag comparison

3. **Seasonal statistical support**

   - onset vs seasonal non-onset reference design
   - day-0 significance maps
   - selected precursor significance at lag −1
   - fraction of significant area summary

4. **Focused physical consistency support**

   - seasonal precipitation background
   - focused February / May / July support
   - compact interpretation notes

5. **ENSO modulation**

   - CPC RONI phase assignment by season-year
   - onset frequency by phase
   - onset-day synoptic stories by phase
   - WHO exceedance diagnostics by phase
   - simple robustness checks

---

## Seasonal onset framework

Stage 5 keeps the event-definition logic from earlier stages:

> **monthly p90-defined episodes**

and then applies a season-centered final interpretation by regrouping those onset days into:

- **DJF**
- **MAM**
- **JJA**
- **SON**

So the seasonal story is built from monthly-relative extremes, not from a completely new seasonal-threshold framework.

This choice preserves continuity with Stage 3 while answering the request for a more season-based interpretation.

---

## Onset-day framework

Stage 5 uses a strict event-centered rule:

> **day 0 = first day of the p90 episode**

This choice:

- avoids overcounting persistent episodes,
- keeps composites centered on event initiation,
- and makes the lag analysis directly interpretable in terms of precursor vs onset vs persistence.

## Intended contribution of Stage 5

Stage 5 is intended to provide:

- a final **season-centered synthesis** of polluted onset behavior,
- a compact comparison of seasonal event evolution,
- formal support for where onset signals are distinguishable from seasonal background,
- a limited but useful physical-consistency check,
- and an ENSO-conditioned comparison of frequency, circulation structure, and surface burden.

This is the stage that turns the seasonal interpretation developed in Stage 4 into the final thesis-scale answer to **RQ4**, while also helping close **RQ0** and strengthening the seasonal reading of **RQ3**.

---

## Preliminary answers provided by this stage

### RQ0

Stage 5 supports the conclusion that extreme PM episodes over the Valley of Mexico can be summarized through a small number of **seasonal onset-based synoptic stories**, and that these stories are **modulated by ENSO in a seasonally selective way** rather than by one uniform climate effect.

### RQ3

Stage 5 strengthens the idea that background conditions matter beyond event day alone. Seasonal lag composites and seasonal significance comparisons show that some seasons, especially **DJF** and **MAM**, contain physically interpretable onset evolution and background contrast rather than only isolated day-0 anomalies.

### RQ4

Stage 5 addresses this question directly. The main result is that ENSO modulation is present, but it is:

- **season-dependent**,
- **pollutant-dependent**,
- and **metric-dependent**.

The clearest and most defendible ENSO modulation appears in **JJA**, while **DJF** remains physically important but internally split across onset frequency, synoptic structure, and exceedance behavior. **MAM** is the cleanest transition season physically, but not the strongest statistically. **SON** is informative but secondary.

---

## Main seasonal interpretation emerging from Stage 5

Stage 5 supports the following final narrative roles:

- **DJF** = clearest dry benchmark
- **MAM** = strongest transition season
- **JJA** = clearest wet-season contrast and strongest ENSO modulation signal
- **SON** = drying-season context with stronger PM2.5 reorganization than PM10

A compact formulation is:

> **PM10 keeps the clearest dry-season benchmark and the strongest wet-season weakening, whereas PM2.5 retains a more seasonally extended organization, including a notable SON re-emergence.**

## Limitations and scope boundaries

Stage 5 is intentionally bounded.

It does **not** aim to:

- perform source apportionment,
- prove full boundary-layer causality,
- build a full dynamical ENSO-attribution study,
- or reopen all Stage 4 lower-tropospheric diagnostics as central products.

Important stage-specific limitations:

- seasonal composites were built from **monthly p90-defined onsets**, then regrouped by season
- some ENSO bins remain small, especially **warm-phase JJA** panels
- **31 daily observations** near the DJF 2025 edge remained without ENSO label because the lookup table was truncated at 2024
- those 31 daily observations were excluded from ENSO-conditioned diagnostics, but they represent **less than 1%** of the full daily record

These limitations do not invalidate the stage, but they define how strong each claim should be.

Stage 5 is the last notebook in the staged analysis workflow because it closes the remaining open question:

> Are the seasonal polluted-event stories identified earlier only seasonal, or are they also climate-modulated?

By answering that question, Stage 5 completes the analytical side of the thesis.

The next step is no longer another analysis stage, but the transition into:

- full thesis writing,
- integrated Results + Discussion across the earlier stages,
- and later preparation of the complete final defense presentation.
